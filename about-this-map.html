About This Map (For Teachers)

A companion page for the Educator Geologic Map of the Ice Age Trail.

Who is this for? This page is written for Wisconsin teachers, volunteers, and trip leaders who want to feel comfortable talking about the landscape while hiking the Ice Age Trail with students.

Why this map exists

The Educator Geologic Map of the Ice Age Trail was designed to help teachers quickly connect what students see on the trail to the science they are learning in class. You do not need to be a geologist. The goal is to give you just enough background to notice landforms, name them, and talk about how glaciers and bedrock shape today‚Äôs landscapes.

Many Wisconsin students walk right past kettles, moraines, and bedrock outcrops without realizing they are looking at evidence of ancient ice sheets and long-lasting geologic processes. This map and its teacher resources aim to make those ‚Äúinvisible stories‚Äù visible.

How to use the map with students

Before your trip
  • Open the map and zoom to the Ice Age Trail segment near your school or field site.
  • Click an orange trail segment to open the teacher panel on the right.
  • Read the Overview tab for a short, teacher-friendly description of what makes that segment special.
  • Skim the Geologic Concepts tab to see bedrock, glacial history, and key ideas you may want to highlight.
  • Choose one or two activities from the Activities tab to use in class or along the trail.
On the trail
  • Use the overview and geologic story as talking points at trailheads or points of interest.
  • Ask students to look for shapes in the land: closed depressions (kettles), ridges (moraines, eskers), or steep bluffs.
  • Pause at light blue dots (geologic points of interest) to read short explanations of specific features.
After your trip
  • Return to the map with students and have them locate places they remember from the hike.
  • Use the same activities or printable guides for reflection, journaling, or simple models back in the classroom.

Tips for first-time trail teachers

  • Start with one nearby segment and one or two geologic ideas rather than trying to cover everything.
  • Use student questions to guide how deep you go into the science.
  • Invite students to sketch the landforms they notice and label them back in class.
  • Partner with another teacher, volunteer, or Saunters leader to share the talking.
